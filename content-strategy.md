## 8. Messaging: Platform Trap

**Core argument:** Business owners selling on TikTok/Shopee are paying 15-25% in platform fees on every sale. Their store page looks identical to every other seller - same layout, same buttons, no brand personality. Worse, the customers belong to the platform, not the business. One algorithm change and reach disappears.

**The position:** Not "leave the platforms." The smart move is to use platforms to find customers, then gradually own them on your own site. Your site is where you keep 100% of each sale and customers come back without the platform's permission.

**Hook:** You're paying the platform to use your own customers.

**Drafted copy:**
> Every sale on TikTok or Shopee costs 15 to 25% in fees. And your store page looks identical to every other seller on there - same layout, same buttons, same everything. Customers can't tell you apart from the shop next door.
>
> Worse: those customers belong to the platform. One algorithm update, one fee increase - and your sales can disappear without warning.
>
> Your own site is where that stops. You keep every sale. Customers come back directly, no platform deciding who sees you and who doesn't.

**Status:** In discussion. Deciding placement and final form.

## 9. Messaging: Template vs Custom

**Core argument:** "Custom design" is jargon customers don't understand. The actual problem to communicate: template sites look like everyone else's, and they can't do the specific thing your business needs. The customer already knows what a generic Shopee page feels like - that's the reference point. A website built from scratch is the escape from that.

**Key insight:** Don't explain what custom design *is*. Show what templates *fail to do* - they can't fit your specific customer behavior (e.g. Messenger ordering, specific order flow). And they look identical to competitors because the competitor bought the same theme.

**Hook:** Your Shopee page looks like everyone else's. Your website doesn't have to.

**Drafted copy:**
> Most agencies build with templates - pre-made sites designed for businesses in general. You put in your name and photos, and it looks like a website. But your customers have seen hundreds of those. They all feel the same. Nothing stands out. Nothing feels like your business.
>
> A template also can't do the specific thing your customers need. If they buy from you through Messenger one message at a time, a template won't fix that. If your products need a specific flow to order, a template can't build it. You end up fitting your business into the software - instead of the other way around.
>
> We build from scratch, around how your business actually works - the right features for your customers, a look that's yours alone, and a flow designed for how people actually buy from you.

**Final EN copy:**
> **Take a moment and picture your ideal website. How it looks, how customers move through it, what it lets them do.**
>
> Most agencies today build with WordPress or pre-made templates. That means they pick a design that already exists, put your name and photos in, and hand it over. It's fast - but the structure, the flow, the features were decided by someone else, for a different business. Whatever you just pictured probably isn't in there.
>
> We build every site from scratch, around what your business actually needs. No pre-made structure to work around. If you can picture it, we can build it.

**Final VI copy:**
> **Thử tưởng tượng một website lý tưởng cho doanh nghiệp của bạn. Nó trông như thế nào, khách hàng thao tác ra sao, nó có thể làm được gì.**
>
> Đa số các đơn vị thiết kế hiện nay sử dụng WordPress hoặc template có sẵn. Nghĩa là họ chọn một giao diện đã được làm sẵn, điền thông tin của bạn vào, rồi bàn giao. Nhanh - nhưng bố cục, luồng thao tác, tính năng đều được quyết định từ trước, cho một doanh nghiệp khác. Những gì bạn vừa hình dung thường không có trong đó.
>
> Chúng tôi xây dựng từ đầu, theo đúng những gì doanh nghiệp của bạn cần. Không có cấu trúc có sẵn nào cần phải làm vừa. Bạn hình dung được, chúng tôi xây dựng được.

**Status:** Copy locked. Can be refined after seeing it live.

## 7. Creative Notes
> Observations and thinking that inform how we work together - not implementation tasks.

**On agents and creative generation:**
Agents can reconstruct from a reference - given a Lusion URL, get 70-80% of the way there. But the original insight that made it feel fresh when it shipped wasn't in any training data. Truly novel design is outside the training distribution by definition.

**On design fundamentals:**
The "rules" in design are post-hoc explanations - someone made something that worked, people described why, that became the rules. The original work didn't come from following them. Designers who teach often can't fully explain what they actually do, because creation and explanation are different cognitive processes. Following fundamentals teaches you to describe good design, not make it.

**On developing taste:**
Taste develops through volume of exposure with intent - not passive consumption but active reaction. The useful habit: when something feels right, write one sentence about the specific thing you're reacting to. Not "this looks clean" but "the text arrives before anything else and that creates weight." That sentence is transferable. The WindingLine and the Lusion scroll technique are examples of this working - isolating a specific mechanism from a felt reaction.

**On the WindingLine failure:**
A good example of the taste-to-execution gap. The vision was clear (scroll-driven line that writes/retracts, loose ribbon feel). Failed because: line finished outside viewport before visible, natural-looking curves broke responsively (too horizontal desktop, too vertical mobile). The wrongness was felt, not measurable - and the iteration loop (describe → agent writes → build → look → wrong in a new way) is too slow for spatially complex problems like this. Giving up on it for now is the right call. Some Awwwards interactions fall into this category.

**On the agent workflow for creative work:**
Works well for: executing a clear direction, articulating why something works, finding where a technique belongs in a structure.
Doesn't work well for: generating novel visual identity, "does this look good" judgment, spatially complex interactions where done-ness is a felt quality.
The practical path: user brings the instinct and references, agent executes and helps articulate principles.

**Preferred reference workflow for animations:**
Natural language descriptions of animations are lossy ("loose ribbon with heavy curves" means something different to everyone). Actual code is exact. Better workflow: find a component that feels close to right, share the URL or code, agent adapts from that rather than from a description. Gap between taste and output closes much faster.

Useful repos for animated React components (stack is Next.js + Tailwind + Framer Motion - all directly compatible):
- Magic UI: github.com/magicuidesign/magicui - scroll animations, text effects, particle systems
- motion-primitives: github.com/ibelick/motion-primitives - lower-level Framer Motion building blocks
- Aceternity UI - Tailwind + Framer Motion, overlaps with existing shadcn setup
- GSAP demos: gsap.com/demos - serious scroll-driven work, adaptable to React

## 6. Visual / Styling

### Problem

**Hero:** Video background + centered text reads as a template. TikTok in-app browser fails to autoplay reliably - when video fails, the whole hero impression fails. Every other direction tried so far feels "casual."

**Site coherence:** Interactive elements each feel cool alone but disconnected together. Four different interaction models:
- Hero: passive video
- Works/BrandingWall: hover reactive
- Boids (ApproachSection): generative + slider-controlled
- DotGrid: cursor reactive

No shared language. Also the boids slider panel (Separation, Alignment, Cohesion, Visual Range) is too technical/developer-facing for a client portfolio.

---

### Hero Options

**Option A - Kinetic text, no video**
- Remove video entirely
- Headline arrives with cinematic weighted reveal (not a fade)
- 3 proof chips animate in below: "700k-follower client" / "Ready in 8 days" / "An Giang based"
- No autoplay dependency, fast, no browser issues
- Risk: might feel too minimal without a visual anchor

**Option B - Work as the hero**
- 3-column asymmetric layout, each column shows a demo running as a silent loop
- Text overlays center column
- Shows the work immediately, identity through output
- Risk: competes with content sections below, the layout carries an "interior design brand" connotation that feels slightly off

**Option C - Kinetic text hero + Lusion-style scroll reveal (preferred)**
- Hero itself is text-forward and motion-strong, no video dependency
- Immediately after the fold: a small contained "window" of the best demo (Cẩm Giang or Barber District) sits anchored in the corner
- As user scrolls, that window explodes full-screen - dramatic reveal moment
- The expansion lives at the hero-to-content seam, not inside the hero - gives it the contrast it needs to land (small → huge)
- Solves: video dependency, coherence (sets a reactive tone for everything below), narrative flow ("here's who I am" → "here's the proof")

---

### Site Coherence Fix

**Unifying thread: everything reacts to you (cursor/presence)**
- DotGrid: already cursor reactive - keep as-is
- Boids: remove slider panel, make boids scatter/attract based on cursor position instead - same language as DotGrid
- BrandingWall: hover zoom already fits
- Hero: whichever option above, make it cursor-aware

**Boids section context problem:** "Flocking behaviour" in an "Approach" section about "crafting the digital standard" is a stretch clients won't follow. Consider: move boids to the /about page (technical audience appreciates it there), replace ApproachSection visual with something that more directly demonstrates craft.

---

### TikTok Browser Video Fix (quick fix regardless of hero direction)
- Add a `poster` attribute to the video element - strong static frame shown when video fails to load
- Hero should never look broken on first visit from TikTok

## 4. /blog (or /notes)

**Current state:** Does not exist.

**Direction:** Build it as a credibility signal, not a content marketing play. Visitors won't read it - it just needs to look alive and professional.

- Route: `/notes` preferred over `/blog` - reads more authentic for a solo dev
- Posts: short (200-300 words), opinionated, specific to niche - not generic tips
- Writing: handled by agents, not manually written
- Cadence: 2 posts/month, consistency matters more than volume
- Languages: TBD
- Future: could wire up with `schedule` skill - agent runs on cron, generates post in your voice, commits it automatically

**Not a priority to build now.** Come back after /works, /services, and /about are done.
